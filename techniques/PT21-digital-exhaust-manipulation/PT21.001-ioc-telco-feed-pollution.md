# PT21.001 — IOC/Telco Feed Pollution

## Metadata

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **ID** | PT21.001 |
| **Tactic** | PT21 — Digital Exhaust Manipulation |
| **MITRE Gap** | Not mapped in MITRE ATT&CK. This technique describes mass submission of false or misleading indicators (IPs, domains, hashes, URLs) to threat intelligence feeds, ISAC sharing platforms, and automated IOC databases — polluting the shared threat intelligence ecosystem to degrade detection quality, cause false positives that exhaust analyst capacity, or mask genuine adversary infrastructure by burying it in noise |
| **Response Priority** | High |
| **Status** | Draft |

---

## Description

An adversary submits large volumes of fabricated, misleading, or deliberately incorrect indicators of compromise (IOCs) — IP addresses, domains, file hashes, URLs, email addresses — to shared threat intelligence feeds (VirusTotal, AlienVault OTX, MISP communities, ISACs, STIX/TAXII feeds). The pollution degrades the quality of threat intelligence consumed by all organisations in the sharing ecosystem, causes false positive alerts that exhaust analyst capacity, and can be used to frame innocent third-party infrastructure as malicious — or conversely, to dilute genuine adversary infrastructure within a sea of false indicators.

---

## Adversary Value

- Degrades the threat intelligence ecosystem used by all defenders — a strategic attack on shared defensive infrastructure
- Mass false positive generation exhausts analyst capacity at scale across multiple organisations simultaneously
- Can be used to frame a target's own infrastructure as malicious — causing their IPs/domains to be blocklisted by partners and customers
- Genuine adversary infrastructure can be buried within thousands of false indicators, defeating indicator-based detection
- Threat intelligence feeds have limited provenance validation — false indicators can be submitted with minimal friction

---

## Why It's Not in MITRE ATT&CK

This technique has no MITRE mapping. It targets the **shared threat intelligence ecosystem** as a strategic attack surface — degrading the collective defensive infrastructure used by all organisations in a sharing community. This is a meta-level attack on the detection ecosystem rather than a technique against specific target systems.

---

## Procedure Examples

1. Adversary creates automated scripts that submit thousands of fabricated IOCs (random IPs, generated domain variations, benign file hashes) to public threat intelligence platforms (VirusTotal, OTX, MISP)
2. IOCs are tagged with high-confidence labels and plausible attribution (e.g., "APT29 C2 infrastructure") to maximise uptake by automated feed consumers
3. Organisations consuming the polluted feed add the false IOCs to their blocklists — causing false positive alerts and potentially blocking legitimate infrastructure
4. Adversary's genuine C2 infrastructure is submitted among thousands of false IOCs — detection is degraded because the signal-to-noise ratio collapses
5. Alternatively: targets a specific organisation's legitimate infrastructure (CDN IPs, mail servers) for submission as malicious IOCs — causing partner organisations to block the target's own services

---

## Countermeasures

### Prevention

| Control | Description |
|---|---|
| **IOC provenance validation** | Evaluate the reputation and track record of IOC sources before automated consumption; require minimum confidence scores and source reputation thresholds |
| **IOC submission rate limiting** | Threat intelligence platforms should enforce rate limits and provenance requirements for IOC submissions |
| **Multi-source IOC corroboration** | Require IOCs to be corroborated by at least 2–3 independent, trusted sources before automatic blocklist addition |

### Detection

| Control | Description |
|---|---|
| **False positive rate monitoring** | Track the false positive rate of IOCs from each source; alert on sources with rising false positive rates — may indicate pollution |
| **Own-infrastructure IOC monitoring** | Subscribe to alerts that notify you when your own IP ranges, domains, or email infrastructure appear in threat intelligence feeds — indicates framing attack |
| **IOC submission pattern analysis** | Monitor threat intelligence platforms for sudden spikes in IOC submissions from new or low-reputation submitters |

### Response

- Immediately report polluted IOCs to the affected threat intelligence platform for removal
- Issue notifications to known partners and customers if your own infrastructure has been falsely listed
- Review and quarantine blocklist entries derived from the polluted feed period
- Engage the threat intelligence platform's trust and safety team for platform-level remediation
- Adjust IOC consumption pipeline to require higher corroboration thresholds from the affected source
