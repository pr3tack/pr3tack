# PT20.002 — Vendor-Influence Campaigns

## Metadata

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **ID** | PT20.002 |
| **Tactic** | PT20 — Sociotechnical Pressure |
| **MITRE Gap** | Not mapped in MITRE ATT&CK. This technique describes orchestrated pressure campaigns targeting organisations to adopt specific insecure SaaS products, cloud services, or technologies — through coordinated analyst reports, peer pressure, or fabricated case studies — that benefit the adversary by expanding their attack surface within the target's environment |
| **Response Priority** | Medium |
| **Status** | Draft |

---

## Description

An adversary orchestrates a coordinated influence campaign to pressure a target organisation into adopting a specific SaaS product, cloud service, or technology that the adversary has compromised, controls, or has pre-positioned access within. The campaign uses fabricated analyst endorsements, sockpuppet peer testimonials, coordinated vendor sales pressure, and manufactured urgency to push the target toward adopting a technology that expands the adversary's access to the target environment without requiring direct technical compromise of the target.

---

## Adversary Value

- Achieves access to the target environment through the target's own procurement process — completely legitimate from the target's perspective
- The adopted technology is a trusted integration — it receives network access, data access, and often privileged API access that would otherwise require direct compromise to obtain
- Particularly effective for establishing supply chain access: once the compromised SaaS tool is adopted, the adversary has persistent access to all data processed by it
- Fabricated endorsements and peer pressure are difficult to distinguish from legitimate market dynamics

---

## Why It's Not in MITRE ATT&CK

This technique has no MITRE mapping. It operates through procurement and vendor management processes rather than technical attack vectors — exploiting organisational decision-making as the initial access mechanism. The deliberate orchestration of market pressure to coerce technology adoption choices is a novel adversarial technique not addressed in current frameworks.

---

## Procedure Examples

1. Adversary identifies a SaaS product or technology they have compromised or control, and identifies a target organisation that would benefit from (and thus be persuadable to adopt) that technology
2. Creates a coordinated influence campaign: fabricated G2/Gartner Peer Insights reviews, sockpuppet LinkedIn posts from "practitioners," manufactured analyst reports from fake research firms
3. Applies direct vendor sales pressure (the adversary or their agent is the vendor) with artificially low pricing to overcome procurement friction
4. Target organisation adopts the technology — the adversary's SaaS integration receives API access, data access, or network-level access to the target environment
5. Adversary leverages the trusted integration to access target data, pivot to adjacent systems, or maintain persistent access

---

## Countermeasures

### Prevention

| Control | Description |
|---|---|
| **Vendor security assessment** | Conduct independent security assessments of all SaaS products before adoption; do not rely solely on vendor-provided security documentation |
| **Peer reference verification** | Independently verify vendor references through your own professional network rather than vendor-provided reference lists |
| **Procurement security review** | Require security team sign-off for all SaaS adoptions that involve access to sensitive data or privileged API access |

### Detection

| Control | Description |
|---|---|
| **Review source analysis** | When evaluating SaaS products, analyse the source and recency of online reviews; flag sudden spikes in positive reviews for recently-launched products |
| **Analyst report provenance** | Verify the provenance and track record of analyst firms cited in vendor materials; flag research firms with no prior publications or recently-created websites |
| **Vendor access scope monitoring** | After adoption, monitor the actual data and API access exercised by new SaaS integrations; alert on access beyond the declared integration scope |

### Response

- Immediately revoke the compromised SaaS integration's API access and data permissions
- Audit all data accessed by the integration during its tenure
- Conduct a full security review of the SaaS product before any consideration of re-adoption
- Report the suspected malicious vendor to relevant authorities (FTC, FCA, ACCC depending on jurisdiction)
- Issue internal advisory to procurement teams about the specific influence campaign patterns identified
