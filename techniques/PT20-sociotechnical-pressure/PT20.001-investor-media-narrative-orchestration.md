## Description

An adversary coordinates a campaign of investor pressure, media narratives, and analyst commentary to coerce a target organisation into security-adverse decisions — such as reducing security investment in favour of growth metrics, abandoning a planned security control that would limit adversary access, rushing a product release that skips security review, or replacing security leadership with cost-focused management. The technique exploits the sensitivity of public companies and growth-stage startups to investor and media perception as a mechanism for externally influencing internal security decisions.

---

## Adversary Value

- Achieves security degradation without any direct technical attack — the target's own leadership makes the security-adverse decision
- Particularly effective against public companies and VC-backed startups where investor pressure directly influences operational decisions
- Coordinated media narratives are difficult to attribute and appear as organic market commentary
- Long-term impact: security leadership changes and budget cuts take years to reverse

---

## Why It's Not in MITRE ATT&CK

This technique operates at the strategic sociotechnical layer — influencing corporate decision-making through external pressure rather than technical compromise. MITRE ATT&CK focuses on technical techniques; this technique has no equivalent in any current security framework despite being a documented pattern in nation-state adversarial operations.

---

## Procedure Examples

1. Adversary identifies a target company considering a major security investment that would close an adversary-exploited vulnerability
2. Orchestrates a coordinated media narrative framing the security investment as "overspending," "blocking innovation," or "competitive disadvantage" through sockpuppet analyst accounts and sympathetic journalists
3. Coordinates with investors or board members (via social engineering or direct influence) to question the security investment at board level
4. Target company's leadership, under financial pressure, reduces or abandons the planned security investment
5. Adversary retains access to the vulnerability that would have been closed

---

## Countermeasures

### Prevention

| Control | Description |
|---|---|
| **Security investment business case documentation** | Document the risk reduction value of security investments in business terms; provide board-level visibility into what attack surface each investment addresses |
| **Security leadership independence** | Ensure CISO reports to CEO or Board directly — not through CFO or CTO — to insulate security decisions from purely financial pressure |
| **Media narrative monitoring** | Monitor for coordinated media and analyst narratives targeting your security posture or leadership |

### Detection

| Control | Description |
|---|---|
| **Coordinated commentary pattern analysis** | Monitor for sudden clusters of investor, media, and analyst commentary targeting specific security investments or security leadership — flag unusual coordination patterns |
| **Source credibility assessment** | Assess the credibility and prior track record of sources driving security-critical narratives; flag newly-created analyst accounts or publications |

### Response

- Engage legal counsel to assess whether the coordinated narrative constitutes market manipulation
- Provide the board with a clear, quantified security risk analysis to counter the coordinated narrative
- Engage trusted external security advisors to provide independent validation of the security investment rationale
- Report suspected coordinated influence operations to relevant regulatory authorities (SEC, FCA)
