# PT16.001 — Standards/Comments Manipulation

---

## Description

An adversary infiltrates or influences the comment and review processes of technical standards bodies (IETF, ISO, NIST, W3C), security framework development processes (CIS Controls, NIST CSF, SOC 2 criteria), or regulatory consultation processes to introduce subtle weaknesses, deliberate ambiguities, or adversary-favourable provisions into published standards. Adopted standards are then implemented by thousands of organisations, embedding the adversary-introduced weakness at ecosystem scale — far beyond what direct technical compromise could achieve.

---

## Adversary Value

- Ecosystem-scale impact: a weakness in a widely-adopted standard propagates to every implementation automatically
- Extremely long dwell time — standards remain in effect for years; a weakness introduced today may be exploited for a decade
- Plausible deniability: participation in standards processes is legitimate and the adversary's contributions appear as good-faith technical input
- Nation-state level technique — the investment required is significant but the payoff is proportionally vast

---

## Why It's Not in MITRE ATT&CK

This technique has no MITRE mapping. It operates at the governance and standards layer — above the technical attack surface that MITRE ATT&CK addresses. PR3TACK's explicit focus on underexplored, forward-looking threats makes this a natural inclusion.

---

## Procedure Examples

1. Adversary establishes a credible standards-body persona over 2–3 years through legitimate technical contributions
2. Participates in the comment process for a cryptographic standard, security framework, or protocol specification
3. Submits technically-sound but subtly-weakened proposals (e.g., reduced key size minimums, ambiguous certificate validation requirements, optional security controls presented as equivalent to mandatory ones)
4. Builds coalitions with other participants to champion the weakened provision
5. If adopted, the weakness propagates to all implementations of the standard — exploitable by the adversary or any party who subsequently discovers it

---

## Countermeasures

### Prevention

| Control | Description |
|---|---|
| **Standards participation transparency** | Require disclosure of organisational affiliations for all standards body participants; support IETF/ISO conflict of interest policies |
| **Independent security review of standards** | Commission independent cryptographic and security review of draft standards before adoption; do not rely solely on the standards body review process |
| **Multi-stakeholder review** | Ensure standards are reviewed by adversarially-minded security researchers, not only by industry insiders |

### Detection

| Control | Description |
|---|---|
| **Standards weakness monitoring** | Monitor published standards for provisions that weaken existing security requirements or introduce ambiguity in mandatory controls |
| **Comment origin analysis** | Track the organisational affiliations and geographic patterns of standards comment submissions; flag unusual clusters of support for weakening provisions |

### Response

- Engage the standards body to flag specific weakness concerns through formal comment processes
- Coordinate with the security research community to publish analysis of the suspected weakness
- Where possible, implement the stronger interpretation of ambiguous standards provisions rather than the minimum required
- Engage national standards bodies (NIST, BSI, ANSSI) to review and address suspected adversarial manipulation
