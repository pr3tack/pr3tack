# PT14.002 — Anticipatory Brand/Domain Squatting

## Metadata

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **ID** | PT14.002 |
| **Tactic** | PT14 — Pre-Positioning |
| **MITRE Gap** | MITRE T1583.001 (Acquire Infrastructure: Domains) covers domain registration for C2; this technique specifically describes the **anticipatory** registration of look-alike domains before a target organisation is selected — registering typosquats, combosquats, and brand-adjacent domains during the pre-positioning phase to have aged, reputable domains ready for phishing, credential harvesting, and BEC at the point of targeting |
| **Response Priority** | High |
| **Status** | Draft |

---

## Description

An adversary registers look-alike domains, typosquats, and brand-adjacent domains for target organisations well in advance of active operations — allowing the domains to age and accumulate a clean reputation score before being activated for phishing, credential harvesting, or business email compromise. Newly registered domains are frequently flagged by email security gateways and web proxies; anticipatory registration months in advance allows these controls to be bypassed by presenting an aged, reputationally clean domain at the point of attack.

---

## Adversary Value

- Aged domains bypass email gateway "newly registered domain" reputation filters that flag domains < 30–90 days old
- Look-alike domains pre-registered before a merger, acquisition, or product launch can be activated at precisely the right moment
- Domain registration is cheap, scalable, and automated — hundreds of variations can be registered simultaneously
- Pre-registered domains with matching SSL certificates and realistic web content appear indistinguishable from legitimate infrastructure to automated reputation systems

---

## Why It's Not in MITRE ATT&CK

MITRE T1583.001 covers domain acquisition for active C2 infrastructure. This technique is distinct — it describes a **pre-positioning** strategy where domains are registered months in advance for the specific purpose of aging them for later phishing use, exploiting the temporal gap between registration and reputation scoring. The anticipatory and reputation-gaming aspects are the distinguishing characteristics.

---

## Procedure Examples

1. Adversary identifies a target organisation and registers 20–50 domain variations: typosquats (`acme-corp.com` vs `acmecorp.com`), combosquats (`acme-security.com`, `acme-portal.com`), homoglyph domains, and TLD variations (`.net`, `.co`, `.io` for a `.com` target)
2. Domains are registered 3–12 months before planned operations to accumulate age and reputation
3. Adversary deploys basic content (corporate-looking placeholder page, valid SSL certificate) to pass automated reputation checks
4. At the point of operation, domains are activated for phishing pages, credential harvesting portals, or BEC email infrastructure
5. Aged, content-bearing domains pass email gateway reputation filters and web proxy categorisation checks

---

## Countermeasures

### Prevention

| Control | Description |
|---|---|
| **Defensive domain registration** | Register common typosquats and TLD variations of your primary domains proactively; use DMARC monitoring to detect domain impersonation |
| **Domain monitoring services** | Subscribe to domain monitoring services (DomainTools, CSC, MarkMonitor) that alert on newly registered look-alike domains |
| **DMARC enforcement** | Enforce DMARC `p=reject` to prevent email spoofing from look-alike domains |

### Detection

| Control | Description |
|---|---|
| **Look-alike domain monitoring** | Continuously monitor certificate transparency logs (crt.sh) and new domain registrations for domains with high visual similarity to your brand (edit distance ≤ 2, homoglyphs) |
| **Email header analysis** | Alert on inbound emails where the `From` display name matches your organisation but the sending domain is not in your approved sender list |
| **Phishing page fingerprinting** | Deploy phishing detection services that fingerprint credential harvesting pages and alert when a look-alike domain hosts a page mirroring your login portal |

### Response

- File an abuse report with the domain registrar immediately upon identification of a malicious look-alike domain
- Initiate a UDRP (Uniform Domain Name Dispute Resolution Policy) proceeding for clear trademark violations
- Add the domain to your email gateway blocklist and web proxy blacklist
- Issue internal advisory warning employees of the look-alike domain
- Engage your legal team for potential trademark infringement action
