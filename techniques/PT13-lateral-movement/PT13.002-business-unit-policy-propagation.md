# PT13.002 — Business-Unit Policy Propagation

## Metadata

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **ID** | PT13.002 |
| **Tactic** | PT13 — Lateral Movement |
| **MITRE Gap** | MITRE T1484 (Domain Policy Modification) covers AD policy modification; this technique specifically addresses the exploitation of permissive **cross-business-unit policy inheritance** in modern cloud and SaaS environments — where overly broad RBAC policies, shared Entra ID tenants, or permissive SaaS workspace policies allow a compromise in one business unit to propagate access to adjacent units without any explicit policy modification |
| **Response Priority** | Medium |
| **Status** | Draft |

---

## Description

An adversary compromises an identity in one business unit and exploits permissive cross-business-unit policy inheritance — shared RBAC roles, overly broad Entra ID group memberships, permissive AWS Organization SCPs, or shared SaaS workspace policies — to access resources in adjacent business units without modifying any policies. The technique exploits the **existing policy configuration** rather than modifying it, making it difficult to detect via policy change monitoring. The adversary propagates laterally by discovering and leveraging pre-existing over-permissioned cross-unit access grants.

---

## Adversary Value

- No policy modification required — exploitation of existing over-permissioned policies leaves no policy change audit trail
- Modern cloud and SaaS environments frequently have implicit cross-unit trust through shared tenants, shared Entra ID directories, and Organisation-level roles
- Lateral movement path is through legitimate, authorised access — appears as normal business activity in audit logs
- Business unit isolation is rarely tested in security assessments — over-permissioned cross-unit access is a common finding

---

## Why It's Not in MITRE ATT&CK

MITRE T1484 covers domain/policy **modification** as a lateral movement enabler. This technique is distinct — it exploits **existing** permissive policies without modification, making it closer to a privilege escalation/lateral movement hybrid that is not addressed in current MITRE mappings. The distinguishing factor is the absence of any detectable policy change event.

---

## Procedure Examples

1. Adversary compromises a user in Business Unit A (e.g., Marketing) with limited access to Marketing resources
2. Enumerates group memberships, RBAC role assignments, and SaaS workspace policies associated with the compromised identity
3. Discovers that the Marketing SaaS workspace shares an Entra ID group with the Finance workspace, or that the AWS Organization SCP grants all IAM users in the Org read access to a shared S3 bucket
4. Accesses Finance or Engineering resources using the compromised identity's existing group membership — no new access grants required
5. Uses the expanded access to enumerate additional high-value targets and continue lateral movement

---

## Countermeasures

### Prevention

| Control | Description |
|---|---|
| **Business unit RBAC isolation** | Enforce strict business unit isolation in cloud IAM — no cross-unit group memberships or shared roles without explicit approval and documentation |
| **SaaS workspace segmentation** | Ensure SaaS platforms (Slack, Jira, Confluence, Salesforce) have workspace-level access controls that enforce business unit isolation |
| **Regular access reviews** | Conduct quarterly cross-unit access reviews to identify and remediate over-permissioned group memberships and role assignments |

### Detection

| Control | Description |
|---|---|
| **Cross-unit resource access anomaly** | Alert on identities accessing resources in business units they have not previously accessed — using a per-identity, per-business-unit baseline |
| **New resource type access from existing identity** | Alert on established identities suddenly accessing resource types inconsistent with their role (e.g., a Marketing user accessing Finance S3 buckets) |
| **Lateral movement graph analysis** | Use identity graph analysis (BloodHound, Entra ID Attack Paths) to identify and pre-emptively alert on exploitable cross-unit paths |

### Response

- Immediately restrict the compromised identity to its intended business unit's resources
- Audit all cross-unit access events from the identity during the suspected lateral movement window
- Initiate an organisation-wide cross-unit access review to identify and remediate similar over-permissioned paths
- Engage the IAM team to implement business unit isolation controls
